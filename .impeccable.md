## Design Context

### Users
Self-publishing authors, mostly first-timers, writing their first eBook. They arrive uncertain about the process — formatting, exporting, publishing platforms — and need an interface that makes them feel capable without overwhelming them. They're creative people, not technical ones. The job to be done: write a book, make it look professional, and get it published on Kindle/Kobo/Apple Books without friction.

### Brand Personality
**Conversational, Literary, Beautiful.**

- **Conversational**: The interface speaks like a knowledgeable friend, not a software manual. Labels, empty states, and prompts use warm, encouraging language.
- **Literary**: The product lives in the world of books. Typography, texture, and whitespace should feel like a well-designed printed page — not a SaaS dashboard.
- **Beautiful**: Every detail is considered. The tool itself should feel like something an author is proud to use, the way a writer treasures a good notebook.

**Emotional goals**: Creative inspiration, calm, focus. The interface should disappear when writing, and feel like a quiet, well-lit room.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Minimal, elegant, focused. Generous whitespace. Space for writing, not for chrome.
- **Primary reference**: Medium.com — black-and-white aesthetic, writing-first, content takes center stage, typography does the heavy lifting.
- **Anti-references**: Cheap/template-y SaaS pages (gradient blobs, stock photos, badge walls, "trusted by 10,000+ users" counters). Overly techy developer tools. Cluttered dashboards.
- **Theme**: Light mode is the primary brand experience. Dark mode supported. Paper theme to be removed.
- **Color**: Near-monochrome with warm off-white (#faf9f5) base. Single blue accent (#4070ff) used sparingly for interactive states. Let typography and whitespace carry the design, not color.
- **Typography**: Playfair Display for display/headings (literary character). Inter for UI/body (clean readability). Georgia for book content rendering.

### Design Principles

1. **Writing comes first.** Every design decision should be measured against: "Does this help the author focus on their words?" If it doesn't, remove it.
2. **Quiet confidence.** The interface should feel assured, not flashy. No animations for animation's sake. No effects that compete with the content. Let craft show through restraint.
3. **Literary, not techy.** Use the language and aesthetics of books and publishing — not software. Prefer serif headings, generous margins, and the feeling of a printed page over cards, badges, and dashboards.
4. **Accessible to everyone.** Target WCAG AAA compliance. First-time authors include people of all abilities. Contrast, keyboard navigation, screen reader support, and reduced motion are non-negotiable.
5. **Earn trust through simplicity.** First-time authors are already anxious about the process. Reduce choices, use progressive disclosure, and make the next step obvious. Complexity is available but never imposed.
